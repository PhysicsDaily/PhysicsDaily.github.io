1. What are the key characteristics that make a physical standard suitable for scientific measurement? Why are these characteristics essential for scientific progress?
Answer

A good physical standard must be: Accessible (reproducible worldwide), Invariable (unchanging over time and location), Precise (allowing measurements to desired accuracy), and Reproducible (yielding consistent results). These characteristics are essential because science depends on universal agreement about measurements. Without accessible standards, scientists in different locations couldn't compare results. Without invariable standards, measurements would change over time, making scientific laws meaningless. Without precision and reproducibility, experimental verification of theories would be impossible.

2. Why did the SI system transition from physical artifacts to fundamental constants in 2019? What advantages does this provide?
Answer

The transition to fundamental constants provides several advantages: Universal accessibility (no need to compare with a physical object in Paris), Ultimate stability (fundamental constants don't change), Higher precision (quantum standards can be more precise than mechanical ones), and Practical benefits (standards can be realized anywhere with proper equipment). Physical artifacts can be damaged, drift over time, or become inaccessible, while fundamental constants are the same everywhere in the universe.

It fails when the physics involves dimensionless ratios, when multiple competing effects have the same dimensions, or when the relationship involves transcendental functions or complex mathematical forms.

6. Explain the difference between accuracy and precision in measurement. How do systematic and random errors affect each?
Answer

Accuracy refers to how close measurements are to the true value, while precision refers to how close repeated measurements are to each other. Random errors affect precision by causing scatter in repeated measurements but don't bias the average value. Systematic errors affect accuracy by consistently shifting all measurements away from the true value but don't affect precision. Using a dartboard analogy: high precision means arrows cluster together; high accuracy means they cluster near the bullseye.

9. Why is the concept of "exact" numbers important in significant figure calculations, and how do they differ from measured quantities?
Answer

Exact numbers are defined quantities with infinite precision (like 12 inches in 1 foot, or 2œÄ radians in a circle) or counted quantities (like 5 students). They don't limit precision in calculations because they have no uncertainty. Measured quantities always have limited precision based on instrument capabilities and measurement conditions. In calculations, measured quantities determine the final precision, while exact numbers can be treated as having unlimited significant figures.

10. Explain why the redefinition of SI units in terms of fundamental constants makes the system more robust and universal.
Answer

Fundamental constants are truly universal - they're the same everywhere in the universe and don't change over time. This makes standards reproducible anywhere without reference to specific objects. Physical artifacts can be lost, damaged, or suffer from long-term drift. Constants like the speed of light, Planck's constant, and the elementary charge provide more stable references. This approach also allows for potentially unlimited precision improvements as measurement technology advances, since the limitation becomes our ability to measure the constants rather than the stability of physical standards.

12. Why is it important to distinguish between precision and accuracy when evaluating experimental results?
Answer

This distinction helps identify the source of measurement problems and guides improvement strategies. High precision with low accuracy suggests systematic errors that can be corrected by recalibrating instruments or correcting experimental procedures. Low precision with high accuracy suggests random errors that can be reduced by averaging more measurements or using more sensitive instruments. Low precision and low accuracy indicates both types of errors are present. Understanding which type dominates helps choose the most effective improvement strategy.
