Question 1: SI Base Units

Explain why the kilogram was the last SI base unit to be redefined in terms of fundamental constants. What challenges were involved in its redefinition, and what advantages does the new definition offer?

Answer:

The kilogram was the last SI base unit to be redefined because it was exceptionally challenging to link mass to a fundamental constant with sufficient precision. Until 2019, the kilogram was defined by a physical artifact‚Äîthe International Prototype Kilogram (IPK)‚Äîa platinum-iridium cylinder kept in France.

Challenges:

  • Creating a definition that could be realized with sufficient precision (parts per billion)
  • Ensuring backward compatibility to minimize disruption
  • Developing accurate Kibble balances and silicon sphere technologies

Advantages of the new definition:

  • Stability: Unlike the IPK, which could gain or lose atoms over time, Planck's constant never changes
  • Accessibility: The kilogram can now be realized independently in any properly equipped laboratory
  • Consistency: All SI units are now defined in terms of fundamental constants

Question 2: Dimensional Analysis

The period \(T\) of a simple pendulum might depend on its length \(L\), mass \(m\), and the acceleration due to gravity \(g\). Use dimensional analysis to determine the form of the relationship between these quantities.

Answer:

Let's assume that \(T = k \cdot L^a \cdot m^b \cdot g^c\), where \(k\) is a dimensionless constant and \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are exponents we need to determine.

Dimensional analysis:

  • Dimension of \(T\): [T]
  • Dimension of \(L\): [L]
  • Dimension of \(m\): [M]
  • Dimension of \(g\): [LT-2]

Setting up the dimensional equation:

\begin{align} [T] &= [L]^a \cdot [M]^b \cdot [LT^{-2}]^c \\ [T] &= [L]^a \cdot [M]^b \cdot [L]^c \cdot [T]^{-2c} \\ [T] &= [L]^{a+c} \cdot [M]^b \cdot [T]^{-2c} \end{align}

For dimensional homogeneity, we need:

  • For [M]: \(b = 0\) (mass doesn't affect the period)
  • For [L]: \(a + c = 0\)
  • For [T]: \(1 = -2c\), so \(c = -\frac{1}{2}\)

From \(a + c = 0\) and \(c = -\frac{1}{2}\), we get \(a = \frac{1}{2}\)

Therefore:

\begin{align} T = k \cdot L^{1/2} \cdot g^{-1/2} = k \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} \end{align}

This matches the actual equation for the period of a simple pendulum: \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}\), where \(k = 2\pi\) is the dimensionless constant that cannot be determined from dimensional analysis alone.

Question 3: Precision vs. Accuracy

Distinguish between precision and accuracy in measurements. Provide an example where a measurement could be precise but not accurate, and explain how this situation might arise in a physics laboratory.

Answer:

Precision refers to how closely repeated measurements agree with each other, indicating the reproducibility of a measurement.

Accuracy refers to how close a measurement is to the true or accepted value, indicating the correctness of a measurement.

Example of precise but inaccurate measurement:

A student uses a digital balance to measure the mass of a standard 100 g calibration weight five times and obtains values of 92.37 g, 92.38 g, 92.36 g, 92.37 g, and 92.39 g.

These measurements are highly precise (they cluster closely together with little spread), but they're consistently inaccurate (all are about 7.6 g below the true value).

How this might arise in a laboratory:

  • The balance might not have been properly calibrated (systematic error)
  • The balance might have been zeroed with something already on it
  • There might be a consistent environmental factor affecting the readings (e.g., air currents, electromagnetic interference)
  • The calibration weight itself might have been mislabeled

This illustrates why both precision and accuracy are important in scientific measurements, and why calibration against known standards is essential.

Question 4: Significant Figures

A student measures the length of a pendulum as 0.700 m and its period as 1.68 s. The student then calculates the value of the acceleration due to gravity using the formula \(g = 4\pi^2L/T^2\). How many significant figures should the result have, and why?

Answer:

The result should have 3 significant figures.

Explanation:

For multiplication and division, the result should have the same number of significant figures as the measurement with the fewest significant figures.

In this problem:

  • \(L = 0.700\) m has 3 significant figures
  • \(T = 1.68\) s has 3 significant figures
  • \(\pi\) and constants like 4 are considered to have infinite significant figures

Both measurements have 3 significant figures, so the calculated value of \(g\) should also have 3 significant figures.

Calculating:

\begin{align} g &= \frac{4\pi^2L}{T^2} \\ &= \frac{4\pi^2 \times 0.700}{(1.68)^2} \\ &= \frac{4\pi^2 \times 0.700}{2.82} \\ &\approx 9.79 \text{ m/s}^2 \end{align}

Note that if we were to compute this with more precision: \(g \approx 9.78566...\) m/s2, but we round to 3 significant figures: 9.79 m/s2.

Question 5: Standards of Measurement

The 2019 redefinition of SI units fixed the values of certain physical constants. Explain the motivation behind defining units in terms of constants rather than physical artifacts, and discuss one specific example of how this improves measurement science.

Answer:

Motivation for constant-based definitions:

  • Universal invariability: Constants of nature don't change over time, location, or conditions
  • Accessibility: Laboratories worldwide can realize the definitions independently
  • Precision: Modern techniques allow increasingly precise measurements based on constants
  • Future-proofing: The definitions don't limit future improvements in measurement technology

Example: Kelvin redefinition

Before 2019, the kelvin was defined based on the triple point of water (273.16 K)‚Äîthe temperature at which water can exist simultaneously as solid, liquid, and gas. However, this definition had limitations:

  • It depended on the purity and isotopic composition of the water sample
  • It couldn't be realized at extreme temperatures without extrapolation
  • It required specialized equipment to create the triple point conditions

The new definition fixes the value of the Boltzmann constant \(k = 1.380649 \times 10^{-23}\) J/K. This improves measurement science by:

  • Allowing direct temperature measurements across the entire range without reference to water
  • Eliminating variations due to material properties
  • Enabling new primary thermometry methods based on measuring energy directly
  • Connecting temperature directly to energy at the atomic scale

This has particular significance for extremely low temperatures (near absolute zero) and extremely high temperatures (millions of kelvin), where the water-based definition was impractical.
