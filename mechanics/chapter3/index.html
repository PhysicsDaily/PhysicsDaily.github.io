3.1 🌟 Introduction to Force

🔰 For Beginners:

Force is everywhere in your daily life! When you push a door, pull a rope, or feel the weight of your backpack, you're experiencing forces. Force is simply a push or pull that can change how objects move.

What is Force?

Force is a vector quantity that represents an interaction between objects that can cause acceleration, deformation, or both. Forces have both magnitude (strength) and direction, making them fundamentally different from scalar quantities like mass or energy.

Force as a Vector:

F⃗ = |F| in a specific direction

SI Unit: Newton (N) = kg⋅m/s²

Characteristics of Forces

Key Properties:

  • Vector Nature: Forces have both magnitude and direction
  • Contact vs Non-contact: Forces can act through direct contact or at a distance
  • Action-Reaction Pairs: Forces always come in pairs (Newton's 3rd Law)
  • Superposition: Multiple forces can be combined using vector addition

Types of Forces

Contact Forces:

  • Normal Force (N): Perpendicular contact force from surfaces
  • Friction Force (f): Opposes relative motion between surfaces
  • Tension Force (T): Force transmitted through strings, ropes, or cables
  • Applied Force (F_app): Force applied by a person or machine

Non-contact (Field) Forces:

  • Gravitational Force (F_g): Attractive force between masses
  • Electric Force (F_e): Force between charged objects
  • Magnetic Force (F_m): Force on moving charges in magnetic fields

📝 Example: Force Analysis

A book rests on a table. Identify all forces:

  • Weight (W): Earth pulls book downward with W = mg
  • Normal Force (N): Table pushes book upward with N = mg
  • Result: Net force = 0, so book remains at rest (Newton's 1st Law)

🎓 Historical Context:

The concept of force evolved from Aristotle's incorrect theories (heavier objects fall faster) through Galileo's insights (inertia) to Newton's revolutionary laws. Newton's definition of force as "that which changes motion" laid the foundation for all of classical mechanics.

🤔 Conceptual Questions

1. Explain why force is considered a vector quantity. How does this affect how we add multiple forces acting on an object?
Answer

Force is a vector because it has both magnitude and direction. When multiple forces act on an object, we must use vector addition, considering both the strength and direction of each force. This means forces in the same direction add algebraically, while forces at angles require trigonometric resolution into components.

2. A book sits on a table. Some students say "no forces act on the book because it's not moving." Explain why this reasoning is incorrect.
Answer

This reasoning confuses "no net force" with "no forces." The book has two forces acting on it: its weight (downward) and the normal force from the table (upward). These forces are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction, resulting in zero net force. According to Newton's 1st Law, zero net force means constant velocity (including zero velocity), not the absence of all forces.

3. Can you have a force without contact between objects? Give three examples and explain the mechanism.
Answer

Yes, field forces act without direct contact: (1) Gravitational force - masses create gravitational fields that influence other masses, (2) Electric force - charged objects create electric fields that exert forces on other charges, (3) Magnetic force - moving charges and magnets create magnetic fields that affect other moving charges. These forces operate through invisible fields that extend through space.

🧮 Numerical Problems

Problem 1: Two forces act on a 5 kg object: F₁ = 20 N eastward and F₂ = 15 N northward. Find the magnitude and direction of the net force.
Solution

Given: F₁ = 20 N (east), F₂ = 15 N (north)

Net force magnitude: |F_net| = √(F₁² + F₂²) = √(20² + 15²) = √(400 + 225) = √625 = 25 N

Direction: θ = tan⁻¹(F₂/F₁) = tan⁻¹(15/20) = tan⁻¹(0.75) = 36.87° north of east

Answer: Net force = 25 N at 36.87° north of east

Problem 2: A 10 kg box rests on a horizontal surface. If the coefficient of static friction is 0.4, what is the maximum horizontal force you can apply before the box starts to slide?
Solution

Given: m = 10 kg, μₛ = 0.4, g = 9.8 m/s²

Normal force: N = mg = 10 × 9.8 = 98 N

Maximum static friction: f_s,max = μₛN = 0.4 × 98 = 39.2 N

Answer: Maximum applied force = 39.2 N

Problem 3: Three forces act on a point: F₁ = 30 N at 0°, F₂ = 40 N at 90°, and F₃ = 50 N at 180°. Find the net force.
Solution

Component Method:

F₁: F₁ₓ = 30 N, F₁ᵧ = 0 N

F₂: F₂ₓ = 0 N, F₂ᵧ = 40 N

F₃: F₃ₓ = -50 N, F₃ᵧ = 0 N

Net components: F_netₓ = 30 + 0 + (-50) = -20 N, F_netᵧ = 0 + 40 + 0 = 40 N

Magnitude: |F_net| = √((-20)² + 40²) = √(400 + 1600) = √2000 = 44.7 N

Direction: θ = tan⁻¹(40/(-20)) = tan⁻¹(-2) = 116.57° (or 26.57° above negative x-axis)

3.2 ⚖️ Newton's First Law of Motion

🔰 Newton's First Law in Simple Terms:

"An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion, unless acted upon by an external force." This is also called the Law of Inertia. Think of it as nature's tendency to resist change!

Statement of Newton's First Law

Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia):

"Every object continues in its state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line unless compelled to change by a net external force."

Mathematical form: If ΣF⃗ = 0, then a⃗ = 0

Understanding Inertia

Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist changes in its motion. It's not a force, but rather a property of matter related to mass. The more massive an object, the greater its inertia.

Key Insights about Inertia:

  • Mass measures inertia: Heavier objects are harder to accelerate or stop
  • Inertia applies to rest and motion: Objects resist both starting and stopping
  • Direction matters: Objects resist changes in direction, not just speed
  • No force needed for constant motion: Uniform motion is natural

Reference Frames and Galilean Relativity

Newton's First Law is only valid in inertial reference frames - frames that are not accelerating. In non-inertial frames (like a accelerating car), objects appear to accelerate without any apparent force due to fictitious forces.

📝 Example: Coffee Cup in a Car

Scenario: A coffee cup sits on your car's dashboard as you drive at constant 60 mph, then suddenly brake.

Analysis:

  • Constant speed: Cup has zero acceleration (First Law satisfied)
  • During braking: Car decelerates, but cup continues forward due to inertia
  • Result: Cup appears to "slide forward" - it's actually maintaining its original motion while the car slows down

🎓 Galilean Principle:

Galileo first recognized that uniform motion is as natural as rest. This revolutionary idea contradicted Aristotelian physics, which claimed that objects naturally come to rest. Newton's First Law formalized Galileo's insight and established the concept of inertial reference frames.

🤔 Conceptual Questions

1. A hockey puck slides across frictionless ice at constant velocity. According to Newton's First Law, what can you conclude about the forces acting on the puck?
Answer

Since the puck moves at constant velocity (zero acceleration), the net force must be zero according to Newton's First Law. This doesn't mean no forces act on the puck - there's still gravitational force downward and normal force upward from the ice. These forces are balanced, resulting in zero net force.

2. You're in an elevator that's accelerating upward. A ball is placed on the floor and appears to "roll backward" without being touched. Does this violate Newton's First Law? Explain.
Answer

This doesn't violate Newton's First Law because the elevator is not an inertial reference frame. From an inertial reference frame outside the elevator, the ball maintains its original position while the elevator floor accelerates forward beneath it. Newton's laws only apply in inertial reference frames.

3. Aristotle believed that objects naturally come to rest. How does Newton's First Law contradict this, and what everyday experience seems to support Aristotle's view? Why is this experience misleading?
Answer

Newton's First Law states that objects in motion stay in motion unless acted upon by a force. Everyday experience (like a ball rolling and stopping) seems to support Aristotle because we don't usually notice friction. The ball stops because friction exerts a force opposing its motion, not because rest is its "natural state." In space, with no friction, objects continue moving indefinitely.

🧮 Numerical Problems

Problem 1: A 1500 kg car travels at constant 30 m/s on a horizontal road. What is the net force acting on the car? If the engine provides 800 N forward, what is the friction force?
Solution

Given: m = 1500 kg, v = 30 m/s (constant), F_engine = 800 N

Since velocity is constant: a = 0, therefore ΣF = 0 (Newton's First Law)

Force analysis: F_engine - f_friction = 0

Friction force: f_friction = F_engine = 800 N (opposing motion)

Answer: Net force = 0 N, Friction force = 800 N backward

Problem 2: A book slides across a table with initial velocity 5 m/s and comes to rest after traveling 2 m. If the book's mass is 0.5 kg, find the coefficient of kinetic friction.
Solution

Given: v₀ = 5 m/s, v = 0 m/s, s = 2 m, m = 0.5 kg

Find acceleration: v² = v₀² + 2as → 0 = 25 + 2a(2) → a = -6.25 m/s²

Apply Newton's Second Law: F_net = ma = 0.5 × (-6.25) = -3.125 N

Friction force: f = μₖN = μₖmg = 3.125 N

Coefficient of friction: μₖ = 3.125/(0.5 × 9.8) = 0.638

3.3 🚀 Newton's Second Law of Motion

🔰 Newton's Second Law in Simple Terms:

"The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass." In other words: more force = more acceleration, more mass = less acceleration.

Statement of Newton's Second Law

Newton's Second Law:

F⃗_net = ma⃗

where F⃗_net is the vector sum of all forces

Understanding the Relationship

Newton's Second Law establishes a quantitative relationship between force, mass, and acceleration. This law allows us to predict the motion of objects when forces are known, or to determine forces when motion is observed.

Key Aspects of F = ma:

  • Vector equation: Force and acceleration are in the same direction
  • Instantaneous relationship: F and a are related at each instant
  • Net force matters: Only the vector sum of forces determines acceleration
  • Mass is scalar: Mass affects magnitude, not direction of acceleration

Component Form

In two dimensions, Newton's Second Law can be written as two separate equations:

ΣFₓ = maₓ

ΣFᵧ = maᵧ

Weight vs. Mass

An important application of Newton's Second Law is understanding the relationship between weight and mass:

Weight:

W⃗ = mg⃗

Weight is the gravitational force on an object

Mass is intrinsic; weight depends on gravitational field

📝 Example: Force Analysis on an Incline

Problem: A 10 kg box slides down a frictionless 30° incline. Find its acceleration.

Solution:

Forces acting: Weight (mg) vertically downward, Normal force (N) perpendicular to incline

Component parallel to incline: F_parallel = mg sin(30°)

Apply F = ma: mg sin(30°) = ma

Acceleration: a = g sin(30°) = 9.8 × 0.5 = 4.9 m/s² down the incline

🎓 Historical Significance:

Newton's Second Law was revolutionary because it provided a quantitative, mathematical relationship between cause (force) and effect (acceleration). This law enabled the precise prediction of planetary orbits, the design of machines, and ultimately led to the space age. It remains one of the most practically useful equations in all of physics.

🤔 Conceptual Questions

1. Two identical boxes are pushed with different forces: one with 20 N, another with 40 N. Compare their accelerations and explain using Newton's Second Law.
Answer

Since F = ma and the masses are identical, acceleration is directly proportional to force. The box pushed with 40 N will have twice the acceleration of the box pushed with 20 N. If a₁ = F₁/m = 20/m, then a₂ = F₂/m = 40/m = 2a₁.

2. An astronaut with a mass of 80 kg weighs about 800 N on Earth but only 130 N on the Moon. Explain this difference using Newton's Second Law.
Answer

Weight is the gravitational force: W = mg. The astronaut's mass (80 kg) doesn't change, but the gravitational acceleration does. On Earth, g ≈ 10 m/s², so W = 80 × 10 = 800 N. On the Moon, g ≈ 1.6 m/s², so W = 80 × 1.6 = 128 N ≈ 130 N. Mass is intrinsic; weight depends on location.

3. A car and a truck are both accelerating at 2 m/s². Which vehicle has a larger net force acting on it? Explain your reasoning.
Answer

The truck has a larger net force. Since F = ma and both have the same acceleration (2 m/s²), the vehicle with greater mass will require a greater force. Trucks typically have much more mass than cars, so F_truck = m_truck × 2 > F_car = m_car × 2.

🧮 Numerical Problems

Problem 1: A 5 kg object experiences forces of 20 N east, 15 N north, and 10 N west. Find the acceleration of the object.
Solution

Given: m = 5 kg, F₁ = 20 N (east), F₂ = 15 N (north), F₃ = 10 N (west)

Net force components:

Fₓ = 20 - 10 = 10 N (east)

Fᵧ = 15 N (north)

Net force magnitude: |F_net| = √(10² + 15²) = √325 = 18.03 N

Apply F = ma: a = F_net/m = 18.03/5 = 3.61 m/s²

Direction: θ = tan⁻¹(15/10) = 56.31° north of east

Problem 2: A 1200 kg elevator accelerates upward at 2 m/s². What is the tension in the cable supporting it?
Solution

Given: m = 1200 kg, a = 2 m/s² (upward), g = 9.8 m/s²

Forces on elevator: Tension T (upward), Weight mg (downward)

Apply Newton's Second Law (upward positive):

T - mg = ma

T = m(g + a) = 1200(9.8 + 2) = 1200 × 11.8 = 14,160 N

Answer: Tension = 14,160 N = 14.16 kN

Problem 3: A 2 kg block on a frictionless table is connected to a 3 kg hanging mass by a string over a pulley. Find the acceleration of the system and tension in the string.
Solution

Given: m₁ = 2 kg (on table), m₂ = 3 kg (hanging)

Let a be the acceleration (downward for m₂, rightward for m₁)

For m₁ (horizontal): T = m₁a

For m₂ (vertical): m₂g - T = m₂a

Substitute T: m₂g - m₁a = m₂a

Solve for a: a = m₂g/(m₁ + m₂) = (3 × 9.8)/(2 + 3) = 29.4/5 = 5.88 m/s²

Find T: T = m₁a = 2 × 5.88 = 11.76 N

3.4 🔄 Newton's Third Law of Motion

🔰 Newton's Third Law in Simple Terms:

"For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction." When you push on something, it pushes back on you with the same force. These forces always come in pairs!

Statement of Newton's Third Law

Newton's Third Law (Action-Reaction Law):

"For every action force, there is an equal and opposite reaction force."

Mathematical form: F⃗₁₂ = -F⃗₂₁

where F⃗₁₂ is force of object 1 on object 2, and F⃗₂₁ is force of object 2 on object 1

Characteristics of Action-Reaction Pairs

Important Properties:

  • Equal magnitude: |F⃗₁₂| = |F⃗₂₁|
  • Opposite direction: Forces point in opposite directions
  • Different objects: Forces act on different objects
  • Same type: Both forces are the same type (both contact or both field forces)
  • Simultaneous: Action and reaction occur at the same instant

Common Examples of Action-Reaction Pairs

📝 Everyday Examples:

1. Walking:

  • Action: Your foot pushes backward on the ground
  • Reaction: Ground pushes forward on your foot (propels you forward)

2. Swimming:

  • Action: Swimmer pushes water backward
  • Reaction: Water pushes swimmer forward

3. Rocket Propulsion:

  • Action: Rocket pushes hot gases downward
  • Reaction: Gases push rocket upward

Why Don't Action-Reaction Forces Cancel?

A common misconception is that action-reaction forces should cancel each other out. They don't cancel because:

  • They act on different objects: You can't add forces acting on different objects
  • Different masses: F = ma, so same force on different masses produces different accelerations
  • Other forces present: Other forces may act on the objects as well

🤔 Conceptual Questions

1. A student claims: "When I push on a wall with 100 N, the wall pushes back with 100 N. Since these forces are equal and opposite, they cancel out, so there's no net force." Identify the error in this reasoning.
Answer

The error is that the forces act on different objects. The 100 N force acts on the wall (from the student), while the reaction force of 100 N acts on the student (from the wall). Since they act on different objects, you cannot add them together to get a net force. Each object has its own separate force analysis.

2. A horse pulls a cart. According to Newton's Third Law, the cart pulls back on the horse with equal force. Why doesn't this prevent the horse from moving the cart forward?
Answer

The action-reaction forces between horse and cart are internal to the horse-cart system. What matters for forward motion is the external force: the horse pushes backward on the ground (action), and the ground pushes forward on the horse (reaction). This forward force from the ground is what propels the entire horse-cart system forward, overcoming friction and other resistive forces.

3. When you jump off a small boat onto a dock, you notice the boat moves backward. Explain this using Newton's Third Law, and predict what would happen if the boat were much more massive.
Answer

When you push off the boat (action), the boat pushes back on you with equal force (reaction). This backward force accelerates the boat away from the dock. If the boat were much more massive, it would still experience the same force, but its acceleration would be much smaller (a = F/m), so it would barely move. The same force applied to different masses produces inversely proportional accelerations.

🧮 Numerical Problems

Problem 1: A 80 kg person stands on a bathroom scale in an elevator. What does the scale read when the elevator accelerates upward at 2 m/s²?
Solution

Given: m = 80 kg, a = 2 m/s² (upward), g = 9.8 m/s²

Forces on person: Normal force N (upward from scale), Weight mg (downward)

Apply Newton's Second Law (upward positive):

N - mg = ma

N = m(g + a) = 80(9.8 + 2) = 80 × 11.8 = 944 N

Answer: Scale reads 944 N (equivalent to 96.3 kg)

Problem 2: A book with mass 2 kg rests on a table. Find the force the book exerts on the table and the force the table exerts on the book.
Solution

Given: m = 2 kg, g = 9.8 m/s²

Forces analysis:

Weight of book: W = mg = 2 × 9.8 = 19.6 N (downward)

By Newton's Third Law:

Force of book on table = 19.6 N (downward)

Force of table on book = 19.6 N (upward)

Answer: Both forces are 19.6 N, equal in magnitude and opposite in direction

Problem 3: A 1200 kg car pushes a 800 kg trailer with a force of 3000 N. If friction on the car is 1000 N and on the trailer is 600 N, find the acceleration of the system and the force between car and trailer.
Solution

Given: m_car = 1200 kg, m_trailer = 800 kg, F_push = 3000 N, f_car = 1000 N, f_trailer = 600 N

Total mass: m_total = 1200 + 800 = 2000 kg

Net force on system: F_net = 3000 - 1000 - 600 = 1400 N

System acceleration: a = F_net/m_total = 1400/2000 = 0.7 m/s²

Force on trailer from car: F = m_trailer × a + f_trailer = 800 × 0.7 + 600 = 1160 N

Answer: Acceleration = 0.7 m/s², Force between vehicles = 1160 N
