üìê Introduction to Scientific Measurement

Physics is fundamentally an experimental science. The ability to make precise, accurate, and reproducible measurements is essential for scientific discovery and technological advancement.

1.1 Physical Quantities, Standards, and Units

Physical quantities are measurable properties of objects or phenomena. They form the foundation of all physical laws and theories. To communicate scientific results effectively, we need agreed-upon standards that are:

  • Accessible: Available to scientists worldwide
  • Invariable: Unchanging over time
  • Reproducible: Can be recreated anywhere
  • Precise: Defined with high accuracy

üéØ Key Insight

Without standardized measurements, a physicist in Tokyo cannot meaningfully compare results with a colleague in London. Standards enable global scientific collaboration.

1.2 The International System of Units (SI)

The SI system, established in 1960, is built upon seven fundamental base units. These base units are defined using fundamental constants of nature, ensuring their permanence and universal accessibility.

1.6 Significant Figures and Precision

Significant figures represent the precision of a measurement. They indicate which digits in a number are meaningful and reliable.

üìè Rules for Significant Figures

  1. Non-zero digits are always significant
  2. Leading zeros are never significant
  3. Captive zeros (between non-zero digits) are always significant
  4. Trailing zeros are significant only if there's a decimal point
  5. Exact numbers have infinite significant figures

üî¢ Examples

  • 123.45 ‚Üí 5 significant figures
  • 0.00456 ‚Üí 3 significant figures
  • 1050 ‚Üí 3 or 4 significant figures (ambiguous)
  • 1.050 √ó 10¬≥ ‚Üí 4 significant figures (clear)
  • 2œÄ ‚Üí infinite significant figures (exact)
